Compress the Behavior,
Not the Information

When an agent's memory gets compacted, does the agent still do the same things?

16 experiments. Qwen3.5 and GLM-4.7 model families. Terminal-Bench 2.0 infrastructure.

The problem

  • Long-running agents outgrow their context window mid-task.
  • Systems overwhelmingly do the same thing: summarize the old history and keep going.
  • Quality is checked with information metrics. Perplexity, QA probes, "are the facts still there?"

What matters is whether the behavior changes, not whether the information survives.

An ideal compaction is one the agent can't tell happened: it acts exactly as it would have with the full history.

Our framing: rate-distortion with a behavioral objective

Rate-distortion theory trades rate (the size of the compressed representation; here, the fraction of tokens kept) against distortion (the distance between original and reconstruction, under a distortion measure that you choose). We keep the framework and choose a different measure:

Distortion = how much the agent's next-action distribution changes.

Test bed: we take real software-agent runs and stop them mid-task, right before the agent's next step. At each stopping point we compress the older history, keep the recent turns raw, and sample the model's next action 8 times with the full context and 8 times with the compressed one.

Acting rate

Does the agent still make a tool call, or does it drift into prose?

Action divergence (D)

Distance between the two next-action distributions. 0 = identical behavior, 1 = completely different.

Ground-truth agreement

The traces record what the agent actually did next.

Sampling is non-deterministic: the same context sampled twice already differs by about 0.3. We account for this by measuring the noise floor first and only reporting effects above it.

Validating the metric against logged actions

A divergence metric computed against the model's own full-context output is self-referential. To ground it externally, we use the fact that each trace records the action the original agent actually took at every stopping point.

full context (100% kept): 73% agreement
keep recent: newest half of old history + raw slice (56% kept): 67%
LLM summary + raw slice (19% kept): 30%

Note the budgets are not matched here: the summary condition keeps far fewer tokens (19% vs 56%).

The two scores agree: compression methods that our metric D rates as more damaging also reproduce the agent's recorded action less often. Since D matches this independent reference, we can trust what it measures.

We also tested whether the full-context reference point is stable. Keeping the content identical but adding irrelevant filler text still changed the model's actions (drift 0.56 padded vs 0.60 truncated). Context length by itself shifts behavior, so training targets should be averaged over several context lengths.

exp8, exp3.

Finding 1: content types differ sharply in behavioral value

Token composition of the histories, measured on our example set: tool calls (commands the agent issued) 25%, observations (environment outputs) 36%, reasoning prose 3%.

For each content type we delete it, then delete random spans until every variant reaches the same 50% token budget, so only which tokens differ. Two numbers per condition: acting = how often the model still makes a tool call, and D = how far its next action moved from the full-context behavior (0 = unchanged, 1 = completely different; anything under the 0.33 noise floor is indistinguishable from no change). Baseline is pure random deletion.

random deletion (control): acting 0.81 · D 0.51
delete tool calls: acting 0.53 · D 0.76
delete observations: acting 0.80 · D 0.50
delete reasoning: acting 0.75 · D 0.61

bar width = acting rate

Remove the tool calls and the agent breaks: acting drops from 0.81 to 0.53 and its behavior drifts far from the original (D 0.76 vs 0.51). Remove the observations, the biggest chunk of the context, and nothing special happens: the result looks the same as deleting random tokens (D 0.50 vs 0.51).

The surprise: the biggest, most information-rich part of the context is the safest to throw away, and the small record of past actions is the part the agent actually needs.

exp4 v2 (role-aware), N=24, Qwen3.5-9B; ordering replicated on GLM-4.7-Flash (acting 0.49 vs 0.77 control), across five runs and two seeds, and on our own models' trajectories (acting 0.34 vs 0.66 control).

Finding 2: summaries are the worst option we measured

We measured distortion at four budgets (keep 12.5% up to 75% of the history) for four compressors: LLM summary, keep recent tokens, random deletion, and the block-aware rule from Finding 1.

  • The LLM summary had the highest distortion at every budget. At a 25% budget: summary D = 0.68 vs keep-recent D = 0.49 (noise floor 0.36).
  • Keep-recent was also nearly lossless in acting rate (0.94, vs 0.99 for full context).

Early re-measurement on our own models' trajectories suggests the block-aware compressor may do even better there; still testing, a larger run is scheduled.

exp6, Qwen3.5. Consistent with recency results in KV-cache work (StreamingLLM).

Finding 3: the measurement designs better compressors

We turned Finding 1 into a one-line rule: "copy tool calls and file paths exactly, compress everything else." Then we compared it against the prompts real products use:

naive summary

acting 0.51
agreement 0.33

Claude-Code / OpenHands style

acting 0.61 to 0.67
agreement 0.34 to 0.38

block-aware (ours)

acting 0.80
agreement 0.55

The metric can also pick the best summary. Write 8 different candidate summaries of the same history, score each with D, and keep the one with the lowest score. With that summary, the agent repeats its original action 55% of the time; with a randomly picked candidate, only 38% (p = 0.0004). A larger repeat run on 177 examples with longer contexts shows the same pattern.

exp9, exp11. If the metric can rank summaries, it can also supply training data: keep the good-vs-bad summary pairs and train a compressor on them offline, with no reinforcement learning loop needed (unlike CompactionRL).

Finding 4: delivery format matters as much as content

We hold the summary text constant and change only how it is delivered: injected as a note inside the agent-trace format ("wrapper"), or passed through the model's own chat template. Numbers are acting rates, the fraction of samples that produce a valid tool call:

GLM-4.7-Flash (MoE)

trace-style wrapper: 0.00 (incoherent output)
its native chat format: 0.75

Qwen3.5-35B-A3B (the MoE twin)

wrapper: 0.79
chat format: 0.46. The opposite preference.

Qwen3.5-27B (dense)

fine either way: 0.75 to 0.83

There is no universal compaction format. The same content ranges from fine to catastrophic depending on the model. This also explains why trained-compaction gains fail to transfer across setups.

exp5 (content held constant), exp14 (3 models, 2 formats, 2 sampling settings). The trace format we refer to is the agent-trace format: www.xor.tech/resources/agent-trace

Finding 5: compaction damage is transient

The single-step measurements above capture the moment right after compaction. We also tracked what happens as the agent continues working past that point, following the original trajectory step by step.

  • Immediately after compaction: divergence 0.86, acting rate 0.43.
  • About two steps later: divergence falls to 0.47, acting rate recovers to 0.90.

Single-step metrics overstate long-run harm. The recent raw turns progressively re-anchor the agent's behavior.

This matters for training: it weakens the case for giving early summaries extra credit in RL schemes, and it means compaction quality should be evaluated over a window of steps, not just the first one.

exp10. Continuation is teacher-forced to the original trajectory, so this is an upper bound on recovery. Claims, confounds, and per-claim status: AUDIT.md.

Finding 6: damage is front-loaded, RL credit is back-loaded

When compaction happens repeatedly in one long task, which compaction matters most? We compacted the same history up to three times and measured behavior after each step:

  • The first compaction does nearly all the damage: divergence jumps to 2.3x the noise floor immediately. Further compactions add little divergence; they mainly make the model act less often (acting 0.53 → 0.46).
  • CompactionRL's credit scheme does the opposite: computed from their own equations, the first summary in a four-compaction chain is trained at 13.5% of the learning signal the last one receives.
  • Group-normalized RL (GRPO-style) adds a second distortion: a rollout that compacted four times carries 3.2x the gradient weight of an uncompacted one, for the same outcome.

Our conclusion is to step around the credit problem rather than re-weight it: grade each summary immediately at its own boundary, using divergence as the signal. No terminal-reward credit path, no critic, no group normalization. Finding 5 (damage is transient) supports this: the useful signal about a summary exists right where it was written.

exp7, exp1 (exact computation), exp2 (simulation). Selection by this signal already works (p = 0.0004, Finding 3); whether it trains a compressor is the T4 run currently in the queue.

The grounded benchmark: an honest negative

  • We built a fully offline Terminal-Bench 2.0 pipeline on an air-gapped cluster. 89 task containers rebuilt for Apptainer, models served with vLLM.
  • Our models (Qwen3.5-35B-A3B, int4-quantized) scored 0% Pass@1. Even with 4x time budgets, even on the 25 easiest tasks. Most runs were genuine attempts that ran out of time.
  • The pipeline works. The models are below the benchmark's difficulty floor. So task-level grounding is replaced by the logged-action agreement, which we validated.

A valuable byproduct: 129 trajectories generated by our own models (on-policy data), containing 132 genuine context-exhaustion events with the summaries the agent actually wrote at those moments. We are scoring those directly now.

Takeaway

Keeping the information and keeping the behavior are different goals.
Today's compaction typically optimizes the wrong one.

The gap is measurable above noise. It is grounded against real actions. It replicates across model families and on the agent's own data. And the measure is strong enough to rank, select, and (test pending) train better compressors.
