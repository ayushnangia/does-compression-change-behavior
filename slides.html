Compress the Behavior,
Not the Information

When an agent's memory gets compacted, does the agent still do the same things?

Nobody was measuring that. We built the instrument, tested the instrument, and used it.

14 experiments. Qwen3.5 and GLM-4.7 model families. Terminal-Bench 2.0 infrastructure on Narval A100s.

The problem

  • Long-running agents outgrow their context window mid-task.
  • Every production system does the same thing: summarize the old history and keep going.
  • Quality is checked with information metrics. Perplexity, QA probes, "are the facts still there?"

The unasked question: after compaction, is the agent's next action still the action it would have taken?

Keeping the facts is not the same as keeping the policy.

Our framing: rate–distortion, with a twist

Lossy compression always trades rate (how much you keep) against distortion (what you lose). We keep that framework but change what counts as loss:

Distortion = how much the agent's next-action distribution changes.

Acting rate

Does the agent still make a tool call, or does it drift into prose?

Action divergence (D)

Distance between next-action distributions, sampled 8 times each.

Ground-truth agreement

The traces record what the agent actually did next. That is our answer key.

Noise discipline: the same context sampled twice already differs by about 0.3. We measured that wobble first, and never trust any effect smaller than it.

First, we tested the measuring stick itself

Does our metric track reality? Agreement with the agent's real logged next action:

full context: 73% agreement
just keep recent tokens: 67%
LLM summary: 30%

High divergence on our metric means low agreement with reality. The stick measures something real.

We also stress-tested the reference itself. A padding control showed it is sensitive to context length, so training targets should average over lengths. That critique came from our own group thread. We tested it and it was right.

exp8, exp3. Replicated on the agent's own live-environment runs: 0.74 / 0.67 / 0.30, nearly identical.

Finding 1: the block law

Agent context has three kinds of content. We deleted each kind at the same token budget:

delete tool calls (29% of tokens): halting triples
delete observations (52% of tokens!): same as random deletion
delete reasoning prose (6%): mild effect

Behavioral value lives in the action history, not in the informational bulk.

This is the opposite of what token-importance methods assume. The blocks with the most information have the least behavioral value per token.

exp4. Replicated across two model families, five runs, two seeds. The halting effect also replicates on-policy.

Finding 2: summaries are the worst option we measured

We measured distortion at four compression levels (keep 12.5% up to 75%) for four different compressors.

  • The LLM summary lost at every level.
  • Plain "keep the recent tokens" was nearly lossless (acting rate 0.94, vs 0.99 for full context).

The industry default is the worst thing we tested.

Scope update from the on-policy re-measurement: on the agent's own trajectories, truncation loses its advantage and the block-aware compressor becomes the best curve at moderate-to-high budgets. Summaries stay mediocre everywhere.

exp6 (off-policy 4k) and exp6-coarse (on-policy, tool-level metric; preliminary at N=13, confidence intervals re-running now). Consistent with recency results in KV-cache work (StreamingLLM).

Finding 3: the measurement designs better compressors

We turned Finding 1 into a one-line rule: "copy tool calls and file paths exactly, compress everything else." Then we raced it against the prompts real products use:

naive summary

acting 0.51
agreement 0.33

Claude-Code / OpenHands style

acting 0.61 to 0.67
agreement 0.34 to 0.38

block-aware (ours)

acting 0.80
agreement 0.55

The metric also works for selection. Pick the best of 8 candidate summaries by lowest divergence: agreement with the real action goes from 0.38 (random pick) to 0.55. Paired p = 0.0004.

exp9, exp11. This is the basis for training compressors offline. No critic, no rollouts, no group normalization.

Finding 4: delivery format matters as much as content

Same summary text, different wrapping. Three models, three different personalities:

GLM-4.7-Flash (MoE)

trace-style wrapper: 0.00 (incoherent output!)
its native chat format: 0.75

Qwen3.5-35B-A3B (the MoE twin)

wrapper: 0.79
chat format: 0.46. The opposite preference.

Qwen3.5-27B (dense)

fine either way: 0.75 to 0.83

There is no universal compaction format. The same content ranges from fine to catastrophic depending on the model. This also explains why trained-compaction gains fail to transfer across setups.

exp5 (content held constant), exp14 (3 models, 2 formats, 2 sampling settings)

Where the data corrected us

  • Compaction damage heals. As the agent keeps working, divergence falls from 0.86 to 0.47 and acting recovers from 0.43 to 0.90 within about two steps. Single-step metrics overstate long-run harm. (exp10)
  • One of our ablations was mislabeled and deleted nothing. We caught it because its results matched the control exactly. Fixed and replicated.
  • A "training made things worse" result was fake. The training log showed it never actually trained: wrong model wrote the training pairs, only 4 optimizer steps, and the eval had no statistical power. All three causes fixed. Corrected run queued.
  • A retrieval-manifest idea from our group tested mostly null at this scale. Reported, not buried. (exp13)

Catching our own mistakes is what makes the positive results credible.

Every claim, its confounds, and its status: AUDIT.md. Endpoints pre-registered.

The grounded benchmark: an honest negative

  • We built a fully offline Terminal-Bench 2.0 pipeline on an air-gapped cluster. 89 task containers rebuilt for Apptainer, models served with vLLM.
  • Our small open models scored 0% Pass@1. Even with 4x time budgets, even on the 25 easiest tasks. Most runs were genuine attempts that ran out of time.
  • The pipeline works. The models are below the benchmark's difficulty floor. So task-level grounding is replaced by the logged-action agreement, which we validated.

The byproduct became the prize: 129 on-policy trajectories containing 132 genuine context-exhaustion events, with the summaries the production agent actually wrote. We are scoring those directly now.

Takeaway

Keeping the information and keeping the behavior are different goals.
Today's compaction optimizes the wrong one.

The gap is measurable above noise. It is grounded against real actions. It replicates across model families and on the agent's own data. And the measure is strong enough to rank, select, and (test pending) train better compressors.
